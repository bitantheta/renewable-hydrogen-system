How the Model Works

This page walks through how a simple electrochemical idea was gradually expanded into a full renewable-integrated hydrogen system model. Each section adds one layer of physical realism.

1. Starting from a Single Electrolyzer Cell

Why the Electrolyzer Cell Is the Starting Point

Every hydrogen production system, no matter how large or complex, is built from a single repeating physical unit: the electrolyzer cell. Before it is meaningful to discuss stacks, cooling loops, power electronics, or renewable integration, we must first understand what happens inside one cell when electrical energy is supplied.

An electrolyzer cell is where electrical energy is directly converted into chemical energy by splitting water into hydrogen and oxygen. The rate at which hydrogen is produced, the electrical power required to sustain the reaction, and the amount of heat generated are all determined locally at this scale. Larger systems do not change this physics — they only scale it, distribute it, or impose additional constraints on it.

Key physical law: Hydrogen production follows directly from Faraday’s law. The amount of hydrogen generated is proportional to the electrical current passing through the cell.

This proportionality is exact and universal. Whether the system contains a single cell or tens of thousands, every electron contributes identically to hydrogen formation.

For this reason, current density — the current per unit active area — becomes the natural control variable for electrolyzer operation. System size does not change electrochemistry; it only changes how current is distributed across cells.

Why Voltage Is Not Freely Chosen

The voltage required to drive the electrochemical reaction is not arbitrary. It is set by fundamental thermodynamic and kinetic considerations.

Even under ideal conditions, a minimum voltage is required to split water. This minimum — the reversible voltage — corresponds to the Gibbs free energy change of the reaction and represents the absolute lower bound on energy consumption.

\[ V_{\text{cell}} = V_{\text{rev}} + \eta_{\text{act}} + \eta_{\text{ohmic}} \]

Cell voltage decomposed into reversible and irreversible contributions

Real electrolyzer cells must operate at voltages higher than this reversible minimum because all practical electrochemical processes exhibit irreversible losses. These losses arise from two distinct physical mechanisms:

  • Activation losses — energy required to overcome kinetic barriers at electrode surfaces
  • Ohmic losses — resistive losses within membranes, electrodes, and current collectors

The reversible voltage sets a hard thermodynamic floor. Activation and ohmic losses represent the unavoidable price paid for operating the reaction at a finite rate.

As current density increases, these penalties grow. The operating voltage therefore rises not because of poor design, but because irreversibility is fundamental to electrochemical energy conversion.

Where the Energy Goes

Electrical power input to the cell is given by the product of voltage and current. Only a portion of this power is stored as chemical energy in hydrogen.

The remaining fraction, arising from irreversible losses, must appear as heat within the cell. Heat generation is therefore not a flaw to be eliminated, but a fundamental consequence of operating any real electrochemical system at finite current.

The plots below show how these relationships manifest quantitatively. Voltage, power, and heat all increase as current density rises, revealing the trade-offs that ultimately constrain system-level operation.

Cell Voltage vs Current Density

Voltage increases non-linearly with current due to activation and ohmic losses. Higher current always demands higher voltage.

Electrical Power vs Current Density

Electrical power rises rapidly because both voltage and current increase together. High-current operation is energetically expensive.

Heat Generation vs Current Density

The difference between electrical input and reversible energy appears as heat. Heat generation is unavoidable and grows with current.

These cell-level relationships form the foundation of the entire system model. Stack scaling redistributes current density, cooling systems exist to remove generated heat, and power and thermal limits emerge directly from voltage rise.

In the next section, we examine how the voltage–current relationship shapes feasible operating regimes and begins to impose hard constraints on system-level performance.

2. Thermal Dynamics and Energy Balance

Where the Electrical Losses Go

Electrical losses inside the electrolyzer do not disappear. The difference between the electrical power supplied to the cell and the reversible chemical energy stored in hydrogen must appear as heat within the cell structure.

This heat is generated continuously during operation and accumulates locally within the electrodes, membrane, and electrolyte. As a result, cell temperature does not remain fixed when current increases.

Key idea: Cell temperature is not a control variable. It is an outcome of energy balance between internally generated heat and externally removed heat.

When an electrolyzer begins operating, heat generation exceeds heat removal and the cell temperature rises. This rise continues until the rate of heat rejection to the coolant exactly balances the rate of heat generation inside the cell.

The resulting operating temperature is therefore a steady-state solution, not a setpoint chosen independently by the operator. Any change in current, cooling effectiveness, or coolant temperature shifts this balance and produces a new steady temperature.

\[ \dot{Q}_{\text{gen}} = UA \,(T_{\text{cell}} - T_{\text{cool}}) \]

Steady-state balance between internally generated heat and heat removed by the cooling system

In this balance, the left-hand side represents heat generated by irreversible electrochemical losses, which increases strongly with current density. The right-hand side represents the ability of the cooling system to remove heat, characterized by the overall heat transfer coefficient multiplied by effective area, denoted as UA.

The temperature difference between the cell and the coolant adjusts until these two rates are equal. A larger cooling effectiveness (higher UA) reduces the temperature rise required to remove the same amount of heat.

Why Temperature Rises as Current Increases

As current density increases, hydrogen production rises linearly, but heat generation rises more rapidly due to increasing voltage losses. Cooling capacity, however, does not automatically scale with current.

The result is a monotonic rise in cell temperature with increasing current density. This rise occurs even under perfectly controlled operation and reflects a fundamental coupling between electrochemistry and thermal physics.

Cell Temperature vs Current Density

Cell temperature increases with current density as internally generated heat grows faster than cooling capacity. Cooling effectiveness controls the slope of this temperature rise.

Thermal limits are hard constraints, not performance targets. Above certain temperatures, membranes degrade, catalysts lose activity, and material lifetimes shorten rapidly.

Safe operation therefore requires that current density be limited such that the steady-state temperature remains below allowable thresholds.

Once thermal limits are reached, the system can no longer follow purely electrical optimization. Even if additional power and capacity are available, temperature constraints prevent further increases in current.

In the following sections, we examine how stack scaling, cooling design, and renewable integration interact with this thermal balance to define the feasible operating envelope of the full hydrogen production system.

3. From a Single Cell to an Electrolyzer Stack

Why Industrial Electrolyzers Use Stacks

Industrial electrolyzers are not single cells. They consist of many electrochemical cells connected electrically in series to form a stack. This configuration allows hydrogen production to be scaled to industrial levels while preserving the same underlying electrochemical behavior at the cell level.

Importantly, stacking cells does not change the physics of water electrolysis. Each individual cell still obeys the same voltage–current relationship, generates heat in the same way, and produces hydrogen according to Faraday’s law.

Key idea: Stack-level behavior emerges directly from cell-level physics, amplified by scale. Nothing new is introduced — existing physical relationships are repeated many times.

How Voltage Scales in a Stack

Because cells in a stack are connected in series, the total stack voltage is simply the sum of the individual cell voltages. If each cell operates at the same current density, the stack voltage increases linearly with the number of cells.

\[ V_{\text{stack}} = N_{\text{cells}} \, V_{\text{cell}} \]

Stack voltage increases linearly as identical cells are connected in series

While the magnitude of the voltage increases with the number of cells, the *shape* of the voltage–current relationship remains unchanged. All nonlinear behavior introduced by activation and ohmic losses is inherited directly from the single-cell response.

As a result, increasing stack size does not make high-current operation easier — it simply raises the absolute voltage and power involved.

Stack Voltage vs Current Density

Stack voltage scales proportionally with the number of cells while retaining the same nonlinear dependence on current density imposed by electrochemical losses.

Why Power Becomes a System-Level Challenge

Electrical power consumed by the stack is given by the product of stack voltage and current. Because both voltage and current increase with operating intensity and scale, stack power grows rapidly as systems become larger.

This rapid growth in power explains why power electronics, grid integration, and thermal management become dominant engineering concerns at the stack and plant level.

Stack Power vs Current Density

Electrical power increases rapidly at the stack level as both voltage and current scale, driving system-level thermal and electrical design requirements.

Hydrogen Production vs Current Density

Hydrogen production scales linearly with current density and the number of cells, directly linking electrochemical operation to industrial hydrogen output.

Stack scaling increases hydrogen production capacity, but it does not remove electrochemical or thermal constraints. Voltage losses, heat generation, and efficiency penalties are all multiplied by scale.

Larger stacks therefore amplify both productive output and the consequences of irreversible losses.

At the stack level, system behavior begins to encounter hard external constraints. Electrical infrastructure limits available power, cooling systems limit heat rejection, and component ratings impose safe operating envelopes.

In the next section, we examine how cooling effectiveness and thermal constraints impose explicit operating limits on stack power and define the boundary between feasible and infeasible operation.

4. Cooling as a Hard Constraint

Why Cooling Ultimately Limits Operation

Heat generated inside the electrolyzer must be continuously removed by the cooling system. While electrochemical considerations may favor higher current densities to increase hydrogen production, cooling capacity ultimately determines whether a given operating point is physically achievable.

Cooling systems do not merely influence performance — they define the boundary between feasible and infeasible operation. Once heat can no longer be removed fast enough, temperature rises without bound.

Key idea: Cooling imposes a hard physical limit. Beyond this limit, no amount of control, optimization, or additional power can produce a safe steady operating point.

All electrolyzer components — membranes, catalysts, seals, and structural materials — have maximum allowable operating temperatures. Exceeding these limits leads to rapid degradation, loss of performance, and potentially unsafe operation.

As a result, electrolyzers are designed to operate below a specified maximum cell temperature. This requirement converts thermal behavior from a continuous trade-off into a strict constraint.

\[ T_{\text{cell}} \le T_{\text{max}} \]

Cooling enforces a hard upper limit on allowable operating temperature

At low and moderate current densities, the cooling system can reject the heat generated inside the cell, allowing a stable steady-state temperature to exist. As current density increases, heat generation rises and the steady-state temperature moves closer to the allowable maximum.

Beyond a critical current density, no steady temperature solution exists below the safe limit. At this point, continued operation would cause the cell temperature to exceed material limits, making the operating point physically infeasible.

Thermal Feasibility Limit

The horizontal line indicates the maximum allowable cell temperature. Operation beyond this intersection is thermally infeasible, regardless of available power, electrolyzer capacity, or control strategy.

Thermal infeasibility cannot be resolved through better control or optimization. Once the cooling limit is reached, the only viable options are to reduce current density or redesign the cooling system.

This distinction separates soft performance trade-offs from hard physical constraints that define the feasible operating envelope.

Cooling limits are one of several independent constraints that shape system operation. Electrical capacity, renewable availability, and thermal rejection each impose their own boundaries.

In the following sections, we combine these constraints to identify which limit dominates under different operating conditions and how these regimes define the overall feasible envelope explored in the System Explorer.

5. Control and Protection Logic

Why Electrolyzers Cannot Operate Freely

Real electrolyzers do not operate at arbitrary points on the physical performance curves defined by electrochemistry and thermal behavior. Although power availability or operator demand may request aggressive operating conditions, the system must always remain within safe limits.

For this reason, electrolyzers are equipped with control and protection logic that continuously monitors key variables such as voltage, temperature, and current ramp rate.

Key idea: Control systems do not create new feasible operating points. They select the safest possible point within the existing physical constraints.

At any moment, the system may receive a requested operating point driven by available electrical power, renewable input, or external setpoints. This requested current density reflects what the system would like to apply in the absence of constraints.

The applied operating point, however, is determined after enforcing all active limits. When voltage, temperature, or ramp-rate constraints are approached, the control system automatically reduces current to prevent unsafe operation.

\[ i_{\text{applied}} = f_{\text{control}} \bigl( i_{\text{requested}},\, T,\, V \bigr) \]

Control logic maps requested operation to a safe, feasible applied current

When a constraint becomes active, the control system does not shut the electrolyzer down abruptly. Instead, it derates operation smoothly, reducing current density to maintain operation within allowable limits.

This derating trades instantaneous hydrogen production for equipment safety, reliability, and long-term performance. It is therefore a deliberate design feature, not a failure mode.

Requested vs Applied Current Density

The dashed curve represents the current density requested by available power or external demand. The solid curve shows the reduced current actually applied after enforcing voltage, temperature, and protection limits.

Control logic cannot override physical limits. If an operating point is thermally or electrically infeasible, no control strategy can make it safe.

Instead, control systems ensure that the electrolyzer operates at the maximum safe point allowed by the most restrictive active constraint.

As operating conditions change, different constraints become active. At low power, operation may be limited by renewable availability. At higher power, electrical capacity or thermal limits may dominate.

6. Renewable Power Integration

Why Renewable Power Changes the Operating Problem

When powered by renewable energy sources such as solar or wind, the electrolyzer no longer receives a steady, dispatchable power input. Instead, available power varies continuously over time according to environmental conditions.

The electrolyzer must therefore respond dynamically to changing power availability while still respecting all electrochemical, thermal, and protection constraints developed in the previous sections.

Key idea: The system can only absorb renewable power that lies within its feasible operating envelope. Availability alone does not guarantee usability.

To illustrate this interaction, an idealized daily solar power profile is often used. Power rises smoothly in the morning, reaches a peak near midday, and falls again toward evening.

This simplified profile captures the essential feature of renewable generation: power availability varies continuously and predictably over time.

\[ P_{\text{solar}}(t) = P_{\text{peak}} \sin\!\left(\frac{\pi t}{T_{\text{day}}}\right) \]

Idealized daily solar power profile used to illustrate variable renewable availability

At each moment in time, the control system compares the power requested by the renewable source with the maximum power the electrolyzer can safely absorb. This maximum is determined by voltage limits, thermal constraints, and control logic.

If available renewable power exceeds the feasible operating limit, the electrolyzer cannot absorb the excess without violating physical or safety constraints.

Renewable Power vs Electrolyzer Power Used

The dashed curve represents available renewable power over the day. The solid curve shows the power actually absorbed by the electrolyzer after enforcing electrochemical, thermal, and control constraints.

Curtailment occurs whenever available renewable power exceeds what the electrolyzer can safely absorb. This excess energy is not converted to hydrogen and is therefore lost from the perspective of the electrolyzer.

Curtailment is not a control failure or inefficiency — it is the inevitable result of finite electrolyzer capacity interacting with variable renewable supply.

The balance between renewable availability and system feasibility governs real-world hydrogen production. Increasing renewable capacity without increasing electrolyzer or cooling capacity leads to higher curtailment.

Conversely, oversizing the electrolyzer relative to renewable supply reduces curtailment but lowers utilization. These trade-offs define the design space explored in the System Explorer.

In the final sections, we quantify how these interactions affect hydrogen yield, efficiency, capacity factor, and overall system performance, and use them to compare alternative design choices.

7. Hydrogen Production and System Performance

From Physical Limits to System Outcomes

The combined effects of electrochemistry, thermal constraints, control logic, and renewable variability ultimately determine how much hydrogen can be produced and how efficiently the system operates.

While earlier sections focused on physical feasibility, real systems are evaluated using performance metrics that summarize these interactions into quantities relevant for design, comparison, and economics.

Key idea: System performance metrics are not independent tuning knobs. Each one emerges from physical constraints imposed earlier in the model.

The metrics shown below capture different aspects of system behavior: total hydrogen output, energy efficiency, utilization of installed capacity, and effectiveness in using available renewable power.

Together, they provide a compact but complete description of system performance under a given design and operating strategy.

Hydrogen Produced (kg/day)

Total hydrogen generated after accounting for all electrochemical, thermal, and control constraints.

Specific Energy (kWh/kg)

Electrical energy required per kilogram of hydrogen. Increases when operation is forced to higher voltages or curtailed.

Capacity Factor

Fraction of time the electrolyzer operates near its rated capacity, reflecting underutilization due to renewable variability or constraints.

Renewable Utilization

Fraction of available renewable energy successfully converted into hydrogen rather than curtailed.

These metrics reveal trade-offs that are not visible at the component level. For example, increasing renewable capacity may raise hydrogen output but reduce utilization if electrolyzer or cooling limits become active.

Similarly, aggressive operation may increase short-term production while degrading efficiency due to higher voltage and thermal losses.

The purpose of the System Explorer is to make these trade-offs visible. In the final stage, we use these performance metrics to compare design choices and identify limiting regimes.

8. Design Trade Studies and Scaling Insights

At the system level, performance does not improve linearly with design effort. Increasing renewable capacity, electrolyzer size, or operating aggressiveness each targets a different bottleneck — and only helps until another constraint becomes active.

This section explores how design choices interact with physical limits, revealing where additional investment produces meaningful gains and where it leads to diminishing returns.

Key idea: System performance is governed by the *most restrictive active constraint*. Improving a non-limiting component has little or no effect on output.

8.1 Renewable Oversizing

Renewable oversizing increases the amount of power available to the electrolyzer, raising potential hydrogen production during high-resource periods. However, the electrolyzer can only absorb power up to its electrochemical, thermal, and control limits.

Once these limits are reached, additional renewable power is curtailed rather than converted into hydrogen. Beyond this point, further oversizing produces diminishing returns.

Renewable Oversizing

Hydrogen production initially increases with renewable oversizing, then saturates as system constraints prevent further power absorption.

8.2 Electrolyzer Capacity Oversizing

Increasing electrolyzer capacity allows more renewable power to be absorbed, reducing curtailment and increasing hydrogen output — but only if sufficient renewable energy is available.

If renewable supply is limited, additional electrolyzer capacity remains underutilized, reducing capacity factor without improving production.

Electrolyzer Oversizing

Hydrogen output increases with electrolyzer size until renewable availability becomes the dominant constraint.

8.3 Current Density Trade-Off

Current density determines how aggressively the electrolyzer is operated. Higher current density increases instantaneous hydrogen production but raises voltage losses, heat generation, and efficiency penalties.

Thermal and voltage constraints ultimately cap the allowable current density, defining a narrow band of optimal operation.

Current Density Trade-Off

Aggressive operation increases production but is limited by thermal and efficiency constraints, revealing an optimal operating window.

Together, these trade studies define distinct operating regimes: renewable-limited, capacity-limited, and thermal-limited. System behavior shifts sharply as the active constraint changes.

The System Explorer allows these trade-offs to be explored interactively. By adjusting design and operating parameters, you can observe how the operating point moves between limiting regimes and how performance metrics respond.

This interactive view reveals not just how much hydrogen can be produced, but why the system behaves the way it does.
