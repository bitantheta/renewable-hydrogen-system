Assumptions & Limits

This page documents the key assumptions used in the model and clarifies what the results should — and should not — be interpreted as. The intent is transparency, not to hide complexity.

1. Scope of the Model

The model is a reduced-order representation of a renewable-powered electrolyzer system. It is designed to capture dominant physical and operational trade-offs, not to replace detailed stack-level or plant-level simulations.

Results are most useful for comparative reasoning, sensitivity analysis, and intuition-building rather than absolute design certification.

2. Renewable Power Representation

Renewable input is treated as an exogenous power source with no internal dynamics, forecasting uncertainty, or grid interaction effects. Temporal variability is simplified to focus on system-level constraints.

Energy storage, curtailment markets, and grid export are not modeled explicitly.

3. Electrolyzer Modeling Assumptions

Electrochemical behavior is represented using a lumped polarization relationship. Spatial gradients, degradation mechanisms, and transient electrochemistry are neglected.

Stack scaling is assumed linear, meaning performance scales proportionally with cell count. Balance-of-plant effects are not explicitly included.

4. Thermal Model Limitations

The thermal model assumes a single effective stack temperature and a simplified heat removal mechanism.

Local hot spots, coolant maldistribution, and startup/shutdown thermal transients are outside the scope of this representation.

5. Control and Operational Constraints

Control logic is rule-based and prioritizes feasibility and safety over optimization. Voltage limits, temperature limits, and ramp-rate limits are enforced deterministically.

Advanced predictive control, economic dispatch, and degradation-aware control strategies are not included.

6. How to Interpret the Results

The model outputs should be interpreted as feasible operating trends, not exact production guarantees.

When a parameter change does not increase hydrogen output, it indicates that another constraint has become limiting — not that the model is malfunctioning.

Within these assumptions, the model provides a physically grounded and transparent framework for understanding renewable hydrogen system behavior.
